1.1 True Digital Orthophoto Map (TDOM) Generation

Traditional methods for creating True Digital Orthophoto Maps (TDOMs) use complicated techniques to figure out which parts of the scene should be visible in the final image. Because of this, the maps often contain problems like blurry double images (ghosting) or distorted building shapes. Newer approaches, such as Ortho-NeRF, fix many of these issues by using a more advanced rendering technique that removes perspective distortion. However, this method requires very high computing power and takes a long time to process. To get accurate results without needing so much computation, simpler and faster methods like 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) are used. These methods represent the scene in a more direct way, making map generation quicker and more practical. Fused framework like HyGS-TDOM, along with 3DGS with Few-Shot GS, including orthogonal splatting to yield highly accurate, full scene orthophotos \cite{cite1}.

1.3 Shadow Modeling and Illumination Adaptation

While generating a 3D map from the satellite images from over years or months, a major issue with this generation is that the scene changes consistently. The sun's angle shift causes the shadows to move around the object, and also the temporary objects, like cars, appear and disappear within the images \cite{cite5}. This makes it confusing for the 3D mapping software. To fix this issue, new smart models like S-NeRF and SatGS were developed, which did not just focus on memorizing the pictures, but also calculated the sun’s position to separate the true shape of the building from its temporary shadow  \cite{cite5, cite4}. Some of the well-designed tools can even adapt to the visual changes in seasonal variations like winter snow, and automatically ignore moving cars so they do not distort the map \cite{cite5}. To train these AI models, researchers created a massive S-EO dataset, which provided thousands of examples of perfectly mapped shadows that act as a training guide to help computers build flawless, shadow-free 3D digital twins of the Earth \cite{cite20}.

1.4 Few-Shot and Sparse-View Aerial Synthesis

When drones capture photos to create 3D maps, they only capture a few photos of the extreme edges along their flight paths. Standard 3D models struggle with this sparse data and often get confused, resulting in the creation of distorted 3D models and messy shapes because they simply lack sufficient visual information \cite{cite1,cite7}. To resolve this issue, researchers are developing few-shot techniques that are capable of building 3D scenes from limited images \cite{cite1}. Latest tools like UP-NeRF use an uncertainty-guided approach to identify which parts of an image the computer is unsure about, learning the clear, easy parts first before tackling the complex details \cite{cite7}. Other methods, like Few-Shot Gaussian Splatting (FSGS), use smart depth-guessing tricks, which helps the computer understand structural distances, ensuring the resulting 3D map to stay sharp and accurate even when visual data is highly limited \cite{cite1}.

1.5 3D Change Detection and Dynamic Scene Analysis

Figuring out what has changed in the 3D environment over time, such as a newly constructed building or a removed tree, is highly challenging, especially when the lighting or weather drastically changes between photographs. Traditional mapping software easily gets confused with these environmental differences and usually requires pictures that are taken from the exact same spot. To solve this issue, a 4D modelling technique, such as a framework called Gaussian Difference, is being developed \cite{cite21}. Instead of comparing flat 2D pictures, this tool builds a 3D digital model of the area both before and after the change occurs. It automatically acts like a digital supervisor, assigning tracking IDs to every individual object and comparing them across time. This allows the system to easily pinpoint true structural changes from any viewing angle, completely ignoring misleading differences caused by shifting shadows or varying sunlight \cite{cite21}.
